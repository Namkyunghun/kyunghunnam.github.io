Research statement

What makes a good optimizer?

I study this question through preconditioned optimization. My current view is that a good preconditioner can make a good optimizer by reshaping gradients according to curvature, covariance, noise geometry, and loss-landscape structure.

Working hypothesis

A good optimizer is largely a good preconditioner.

My central research question is: what makes a good optimizer? I currently study this through the hypothesis that optimizer quality depends strongly on how well a method constructs and applies a preconditioner: a transformation that rescales gradients using curvature, covariance, noise, or structured second-moment information so that training becomes better conditioned, more stable, and more computationally efficient.

  • AdamW · Shampoo · SOAP
  • Hessian / Fisher / covariance
  • Gauss–Newton structure
  • low-cost matrix computation
  • loss-landscape diagnostics

Research themes

The main topics all serve the same optimizer question.

Each theme supports the design of preconditioned optimization methods that are geometrically meaningful, computationally feasible, and useful in real training.

Preconditioner construction

Study how diagonal, block, Kronecker, low-rank, and spectral approximations turn gradient information into effective update directions.

Curvature and statistical structure

Analyze Hessian, Fisher, Gauss–Newton, empirical covariance, and gradient second moments to decide what a preconditioner should approximate.

Numerical efficiency

Reduce the cost of inverse roots, eigendecompositions, matrix updates, damping schedules, and stale-preconditioner reuse.

Loss-landscape difficulty

Diagnose ill-conditioning, anisotropic curvature, eigenspace drift, and noisy gradient geometry that slow training.

Stability and robustness

Understand when preconditioning destabilizes training and how damping, normalization, clipping, and refresh rules can control that risk.

Training-cost validation

Evaluate whether a better preconditioner actually improves wall-clock performance, memory use, sensitivity, and reproducibility.

From diagnosis to design

The research workflow is organized around preconditioning.

Step 1

Diagnose why training is hard

Start from loss-landscape and gradient diagnostics: ill-conditioning, anisotropy, eigenspace drift, stochastic noise, and unstable update scales.

Step 2

Identify useful structure

Use Hessian, Fisher, covariance, Gauss–Newton, and second-moment analyses to isolate structure that can plausibly improve optimization.

Step 3

Design the preconditioner

Translate the structure into an implementable gradient transformation: diagonal AdamW-style scaling, Kronecker Shampoo factors, SOAP-style eigenspaces, or hybrid approximations.

Step 4

Make the computation cheap enough

Reduce the cost of inverse roots, eigendecompositions, refresh schedules, damping, factor updates, and stale reuse so that the method remains practical.

Step 5

Validate optimizer quality

Check whether the preconditioner improves convergence, stability, hyperparameter sensitivity, wall-clock time, and memory behavior rather than only one isolated metric.

Methodology

Mathematical analysis paired with numerical design

I want optimizer theory, matrix computation, and empirical diagnostics to constrain each other. A preconditioner should be mathematically motivated, numerically affordable, and visibly useful in real training runs.

  • State what geometry or statistic the preconditioner is meant to approximate.
  • Analyze Hessian, Fisher, covariance, Gauss–Newton, or second-moment structure explicitly.
  • Track the computational cost of inverse roots, eigendecompositions, damping, and refresh rules.
  • Connect loss-landscape diagnostics to concrete preconditioner design choices.

Current questions

Questions guiding my reading and experimentation

  • What mathematical property makes a preconditioner good for deep learning?
  • When are diagonal, Kronecker, spectral, or covariance-based preconditioners worth their cost?
  • How can Hessian/Fisher/Gauss–Newton spectra explain difficult training regimes?
  • How can inverse-root and eigendecomposition costs be reduced without destroying preconditioner fidelity?
  • How should damping, staleness, and eigenspace refreshes be controlled automatically?
