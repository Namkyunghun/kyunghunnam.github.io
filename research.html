Research statement

A good optimizer is a training recipe, not only an update rule.

I study optimization for modern deep learning through the lens of total training cost: reaching target quality with fewer steps, faster step time, lower memory overhead, and fewer failures across realistic systems.

ICML 2026 FOAM accepted paper
3 evaluation axes: iteration, wall-clock, stability
6 training-stack layers from update rules to automation

Working definition

Optimizer quality should be measured as total training cost.

For me, a good optimizer is the combination of update rule, schedule, clipping, normalization, parameterization, precision, and systems choices that reaches the same target quality at lower total cost. This is why I treat training optimization as a stack rather than a single algorithmic component.

  • fewer steps to target
  • faster wall-clock time
  • fewer NaNs / restarts / OOMs
  • lower memory and communication cost

Research themes

The main topics that grow out of this definition.

My research agenda is organized around how to define, measure, explain, and improve training optimization under realistic constraints.

Total-cost evaluation

Build criteria that combine steps-to-target, wall-clock time, token efficiency, memory, and failure modes.

Strong baselines

Understand when well-tuned AdamW-style baselines already solve most of the problem and what evidence is needed before claiming improvement.

Second-order and geometric methods

Study when curvature-aware or geometry-inspired updates reduce total cost rather than only iteration count.

Stability recipes

Analyze warmup, WSD, clipping, EMA, normalization, and residual scaling as central parts of optimization.

Scale transfer and batch regimes

Connect parameterization, hyperparameter transfer, critical batch size, and compute-efficient scaling across model sizes.

Systems-aware training

Bring together low precision, kernels, distributed sharding, long-context training, and PEFT with optimizer design.

Training optimization stack

The optimizer sits inside a larger research map.

Layer 1

Update rules and optimizer families

SGD, AdamW, adaptive methods, Shampoo, SOAP, Muon-style updates, and geometry-aware optimizers define local training dynamics.

Layer 2

Schedules and stability controls

Warmup, decay, WSD, clipping, EMA, weight decay, and damping often determine whether an update rule is usable at scale.

Layer 3

Parameterization and normalization

Scale, initialization, residual structure, normalization, and batch regime determine which hyperparameters transfer.

Layer 4

Systems and distributed execution

Precision, kernels, memory sharding, and communication overhead decide whether algorithmic gains are visible in wall-clock time.

Layer 5

Adaptation and efficient fine-tuning

LoRA, QLoRA, and related PEFT methods connect optimizer research to low-memory adaptation and deployment constraints.

Layer 6

Automation and meta-optimization

BOHB, PBT, and LLM-assisted experiment design raise questions about reproducibility, safety, and search efficiency.

Methodology

Mathematical analysis paired with realistic evaluation

I want theory and practice to constrain each other. My goal is not only to prove convergence or show one benchmark win, but to understand why an optimizer helps and when that help remains visible in real training runs.

  • State the optimization model and stochastic assumptions explicitly.
  • Separate iteration efficiency from wall-clock efficiency.
  • Report stability failures, sensitivity, and implementation cost.
  • Connect proofs to measurable diagnostics whenever possible.
