Research statement

A good optimizer is a training recipe, not only an update rule.

I study optimization for modern deep learning through the lens of total training cost: reaching a target quality with fewer optimization steps, faster step time, and fewer failures across realistic systems.

3 evaluation axes: iteration efficiency, wall-clock efficiency, and stability
6 layers in the training optimization stack, from update rules to automation
1 core goal: the same quality at lower total cost

Working definition

What makes a good optimizer?

For me, a good optimizer is the combination of update rule, schedule, clipping, normalization, parameterization, precision, and systems choices that reaches the same target quality with lower total cost. That is why I study training optimization as a whole stack rather than as a single algorithmic component.

  • fewer steps to target
  • faster step time
  • fewer NaNs / restarts / OOMs
  • lower memory and communication cost

Why this framing matters

Modern training makes optimizer quality a total-cost question.

In large-scale deep learning, an optimizer cannot be judged by a single benchmark win. Its value depends on whether the gains survive changes in schedule, batch regime, precision, kernels, sharding, and distributed execution.

Fair comparison

I care about matched step budgets, wall-clock budgets, and token budgets rather than single-number claims.

Interaction over isolation

Momentum, adaptivity, curvature, schedule, clipping, normalization, and batch size should be studied together.

Systems-aware evidence

Precision, kernels, memory sharding, and parallelism determine whether an algorithmic improvement is real in practice.

Research themes

The main topics that grow out of this definition.

My research agenda is organized around the question of how to define, measure, explain, and improve training optimization under realistic constraints.

Total-cost evaluation

Build criteria that combine step-to-target, wall-clock time, token efficiency, memory, and failure modes.

Strong baselines and optimizer comparison

Understand when well-tuned AdamW-style baselines already solve most of the problem and what evidence is needed before claiming improvement.

Second-order and geometric methods

Study when curvature-aware or geometry-inspired updates reduce total cost rather than only iteration count.

Stability recipes

Analyze warmup, WSD, clipping, EMA, normalization, and residual scaling as central parts of optimization.

Scale transfer and batch regimes

Connect parameterization, hyperparameter transfer, critical batch size, and compute-efficient scaling across model sizes.

Systems-aware training optimization

Bring together low precision, kernels, distributed sharding, long-context training, and PEFT with optimizer design.

Training optimization stack

The optimizer sits inside a larger research map.

I am interested in the full stack that determines whether an apparently good optimizer remains good when models, context lengths, and systems become large.

Layer 1

Update rules and optimizer families

SGD, AdamW, adaptive methods, and newer geometry-aware optimizers define the local update, but they are only the first layer of the problem.

Layer 2

Normalization, initialization, and parameterization

Pre-LN, RMSNorm, residual scaling, and muP-style transfer rules shape the geometry seen by the optimizer.

Layer 3

Precision, kernels, and compile

BF16, FP8, fused kernels, and FlashAttention change step time, memory behavior, and even which recipes are feasible.

Layer 4

Distributed systems and memory sharding

FSDP, ZeRO, tensor and pipeline parallelism, context parallelism, and checkpointing determine how ideas scale.

Layer 5

Adaptation and efficient fine-tuning

LoRA, QLoRA, and related PEFT methods connect optimizer research to low-memory adaptation and deployment constraints.

Layer 6

Automation and meta-optimization

BOHB, PBT, and LLM-assisted experiment design raise new questions about reproducibility, safety, and search efficiency.

Current questions

The questions currently guiding my reading and experimentation.

What is the right objective?

When should an optimizer be judged by loss versus step, loss versus wall-clock, or loss versus tokens?

When does a smarter step stay worth it?

Can curvature-aware or geometry-aware updates remain beneficial once systems overhead is accounted for?

Which stability interventions matter most?

How do warmup, WSD, clipping, normalization, and EMA alter the apparent quality of an optimizer?

How should batch size evolve?

When should batch warmup or critical-batch-size reasoning replace simple learning-rate decay heuristics?

How far does transfer really go?

Which optimizer settings and parameterization rules survive changes in width, depth, precision, and distributed scale?

Can automated HPO stay trustworthy?

How can LLM-assisted experiment design search richer optimizer spaces without sacrificing reproducibility and safety?

Methodology

Mathematical analysis paired with realistic evaluation

I want theory and practice to constrain each other. My goal is not only to prove convergence or show one benchmark win, but to understand why an optimizer helps and when that help remains visible in real training runs.

  • Convergence and robustness analysis for adaptive, momentum-based, and geometry-aware methods
  • Comparisons under matched step, wall-clock, and token budgets
  • Careful tracking of grad norm, clip rate, overflow, memory, and communication breakdown

Research outputs

Evaluation protocols, benchmarks, and reusable tools

I want optimizer research to become easier to compare, reproduce, and extend. That means producing not only papers, but also reporting standards, baselines, and shared resources for the community.

  • Clear reporting practices for failure modes as well as successes
  • Benchmarks that connect optimizer behavior to systems constraints
  • Resources that make strong baselines and fair comparisons easier to build

Research objectives

A roadmap from definition to design.

01

Define the object of study

Formulate “good optimizer” as a total-cost training objective rather than a single-step update rule.

02

Measure it fairly

Develop comparison standards that separate iteration gains, throughput gains, and stability gains.

03

Explain interactions across the stack

Study how schedules, clipping, parameterization, precision, and distributed systems shape optimizer behavior.

04

Design scalable algorithms and recipes

Create methods that remain meaningful not only in theory, but also in large-scale, failure-prone training settings.
