Research statement

Toward principled optimization for modern deep learning.

I study optimization algorithms as both mathematical objects and practical tools, with the goal of making them easier to analyze, compare, and improve.

Core research question

What makes a good optimizer?

The field has no single, widely accepted definition that is simultaneously theoretical, practical, and useful for comparing real optimizers. My work aims to close that gap.

Why this matters

Optimizer design has outpaced optimizer understanding.

The community continues to propose new methods, but it is still difficult to explain which method is truly better, under what conditions, and according to which criteria.

Too many metrics

Benchmark wins, speed, robustness, memory cost, and convergence guarantees rarely align cleanly.

Too little unification

Important ingredients such as momentum, adaptivity, and curvature are often studied in isolation.

Too few bridges

Strong theory often stops before deployment-scale practice, while strong practice can lack explanation.

Research pillars

Three directions that organize my work.

Unified evaluation

Can we define a framework that compares optimizers using convergence behavior, empirical performance, hyperparameter sensitivity, and computational efficiency together?

Second-order information

What role do matrices such as the Hessian, Fisher information matrix, and covariance-based structures play in successful optimizer design?

Scalable implementation

How can theoretically meaningful ideas be turned into algorithms that remain useful when models, datasets, and systems get large?

Current questions

The questions currently driving my reading and experimentation.

Evaluation criteria

What should count as “better” when an optimizer is judged across theory, stability, tuning burden, and compute?

Momentum and adaptivity

When do momentum terms improve adaptive methods in a provable way, and when do they create hidden tradeoffs?

Last-iterate behavior

Can we understand the convergence of actual training trajectories rather than relying only on averaged iterates?

Practical transfer

How can theory-inspired optimizers remain simple enough for real systems and real practitioners to adopt?

Methodology

Rigorous analysis paired with empirical validation

I combine mathematical proof techniques with practical experiments so that theoretical ideas are tested under realistic training settings. This helps ensure that analytical insights translate into usable algorithmic improvements.

  • Convergence analysis for adaptive and momentum-based methods
  • Empirical comparisons under meaningful training regimes
  • Careful attention to robustness, tuning sensitivity, and implementation cost

Open science

Reusable outputs, not only isolated results

Beyond individual papers, I want to contribute evaluation tools, comparison frameworks, and shared research resources that make optimizer studies easier to reproduce and extend.

  • Benchmarks and evaluation criteria that remain interpretable
  • Readable explanations of why specific optimizer components help
  • Resources that support community progress on optimization research

Research objectives

A roadmap from foundations to new algorithms.

01

Theoretical foundation

Develop a mathematically rigorous framework for evaluating and comparing optimization algorithms.

02

Systematic analysis

Study existing optimizers under unified criteria to identify when they are genuinely superior.

03

Algorithm design

Create new optimization methods that perform well according to both theoretical and practical metrics.

04

Community impact

Share tools, benchmarks, and explanatory materials that help accelerate progress in optimizer research.
